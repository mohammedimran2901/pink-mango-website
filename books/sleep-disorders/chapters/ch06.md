## Chapter 6: Circadian Rhythm Disorders — When Your Body Clock Breaks

Your body has an internal clock. It is located in the suprachiasmatic nucleus of the hypothalamus, and it regulates nearly every biological process in your body. It tells you when to wake up, when to eat, when to release hormones, and when to sleep. When this clock is working properly, you feel alert during the day and sleepy at night. When it breaks, everything falls apart.

Circadian rhythm disorders occur when your internal body clock is misaligned with the external environment or with your desired sleep schedule. They are more common than most people realize and can be profoundly disruptive.

### How Your Body Clock Works

The circadian clock runs on a roughly twenty-four-hour cycle. It is synchronized to the external world primarily through light. When light enters your eyes, it signals to the suprachiasmatic nucleus that it is daytime. The suprachiasmatic nucleus then suppresses melatonin production, raising your alertness. As darkness falls, the suprachiasmatic nucleus allows melatonin to rise, preparing your body for sleep.

This system is remarkably consistent. Most people's circadian clocks run slightly longer than twenty-four hours, so daily exposure to morning light is essential for keeping them synchronized. Without that light cue, the clock would drift later and later, causing a progressive delay in sleep timing.

### Delayed Sleep-Wake Phase Disorder

Delayed sleep-wake phase disorder, or DSPD, is the most common circadian rhythm disorder. People with DSPD have a body clock that is shifted significantly later than the typical schedule. They naturally feel most alert in the late evening and have difficulty falling asleep until the early morning hours, typically between 1 a.m. and 4 a.m. When left to their own schedule, they sleep normally but on a delayed timetable.

The problem arises when they need to conform to a typical schedule. Waking up for work or school at 7 a.m. after falling asleep at 3 a.m. creates chronic sleep deprivation. People with DSPD are often labeled lazy or undisciplined, but the reality is that their biology is fighting against the clock.

DSPD is most common in adolescents and young adults, with an estimated prevalence of 7 to 16 percent in this age group. It often persists into adulthood, though it can improve with age. There is a strong genetic component — if you have DSPD, your family members are more likely to have it too.

### Advanced Sleep-Wake Phase Disorder

Advanced sleep-wake phase disorder, or ASPD, is the opposite of DSPD. People with ASPD have a body clock that is shifted earlier. They naturally feel sleepy in the early evening, typically between 6 p.m. and 9 p.m., and wake up very early, between 2 a.m. and 5 a.m. While this might sound ideal for early risers, it can be socially isolating and disruptive. People with ASPD often struggle to stay awake for evening social events and may find themselves waking up long before the rest of the world.

ASPD is more common in older adults, affecting an estimated 1 percent of people over sixty-five. It is less common than DSPD and may have a genetic component as well.

### Shift Work Sleep Disorder

Shift work sleep disorder affects people who work during the night or rotate between day and night shifts. Their work schedule requires them to be awake when their body clock says it is time to sleep and asleep when their body clock says it is time to be awake. The result is chronic misalignment between their internal clock and their external demands.

Shift workers face a unique challenge. Even when they try to sleep during the day, their circadian clock is pumping out alerting signals. Daytime sleep is typically shorter, lighter, and less restorative than nighttime sleep. Over time, shift workers accumulate a significant sleep debt.

The health consequences of shift work are well documented. Shift workers have higher rates of cardiovascular disease, metabolic disorders, gastrointestinal problems, depression, and certain types of cancer. The World Health Organization has classified night shift work as a probable carcinogen. The risk is real and serious.

### Jet Lag Disorder

Jet lag is a temporary circadian rhythm disorder caused by rapid travel across multiple time zones. When you fly from New York to London, your body clock is still on New York time, but the environment says it is morning. Your body wants to sleep, but the sun says wake up. The result is fatigue, insomnia, difficulty concentrating, digestive problems, and a general feeling of being out of sync.

Jet lag typically resolves on its own at a rate of about one day per time zone crossed. Eastward travel is generally harder than westward travel because it requires advancing your clock — a more difficult adjustment for most people.

### Non-24-Hour Sleep-Wake Disorder

Non-24-hour sleep-wake disorder is a rare circadian rhythm disorder in which the body clock runs on a cycle longer than twenty-four hours. Each day, the person's natural sleep time shifts later and later, cycling through all possible sleep times over the course of weeks or months.

This disorder is most common in people who are totally blind. Without light perception, the circadian clock cannot be synchronized to the twenty-four-hour day. It affects about 50 to 75 percent of blind individuals. It can also occur in sighted people, though this is rare.

### Treating Circadian Rhythm Disorders

The primary treatment for circadian rhythm disorders is timed light exposure. Light is the most powerful synchronizer of the circadian clock, and careful timing of light exposure can shift the clock in the desired direction.

For DSPD, morning bright light therapy is the cornerstone of treatment. Exposure to bright light shortly after waking helps advance the clock, making it easier to fall asleep earlier in the evening. Light boxes that deliver 10,000 lux of cool white light are commonly used. Treatment typically involves thirty to sixty minutes of light exposure immediately upon waking.

Melatonin supplementation can also help. A small dose of melatonin, typically 0.5 to 3 milligrams, taken a few hours before the desired bedtime, can help advance the clock. The timing is critical — melatonin taken too early or too late can have the opposite effect.

For shift workers, strategic use of light and dark is essential. Wearing blue-blocking glasses during the commute home from a night shift can prevent light from signaling wakefulness. Creating a completely dark, quiet, and cool sleeping environment during the day is crucial. Strategic napping before a night shift can improve alertness during work.

### Practical Strategies

If you suspect you have a circadian rhythm disorder, start by tracking your natural sleep patterns for two weeks. Note when you naturally fall asleep and wake up on days when you have no schedule constraints. This will reveal your natural chronotype.

Then, work with a sleep specialist to develop a treatment plan. Circadian rhythm disorders are highly treatable, but the treatment requires precision — the timing of light exposure and melatonin matters enormously.

### Social Jet Lag

Social jet lag is a term that describes the misalignment between your internal biological clock and the social schedule imposed by work, school, and social obligations. It is most common in people who sleep later on weekends than on weekdays, then struggle to wake up on Monday morning.

The effects of social jet lag go beyond Monday morning grogginess. Research has found that even one hour of social jet lag — a one-hour difference between your natural sleep schedule and your social schedule — is associated with increased risk of obesity, metabolic problems, depression, and lower academic performance.

Social jet lag is not a formal diagnosis, but it is a widespread problem that affects a significant percentage of the population. The solution is consistency — aligning your sleep schedule as much as possible across all days of the week. This is easier said than done, but even reducing the weekend delay from two hours to one hour can make a difference.

### The Genetics of Chronotype

Your chronotype — whether you are a morning person, evening person, or somewhere in between — is largely determined by your genes. Research has identified hundreds of genetic variants that influence chronotype, many of them affecting the core circadian clock genes.

These genetic differences explain why some people naturally wake up at 5 a.m. while others struggle to function before 10 a.m. They are not a matter of discipline or character. They are a matter of biology.

Understanding your chronotype can help you optimize your schedule. If you are a natural evening type, forcing yourself to wake up at 5 a.m. for a 6 a.m. workout is unlikely to be sustainable. Working with your chronotype rather than against it produces better results.

However, society is structured around morning types. Schools start early. Most workplaces start before 9 a.m. Evening types are forced to live in a morning-oriented world, and this creates chronic circadian misalignment.

### Light and the Modern World

The invention of artificial light has been one of the most disruptive influences on human sleep in history. Before electric lighting, humans were exposed to bright sunlight during the day and near-complete darkness at night. This pattern reinforced a strong circadian rhythm.

Today, we are exposed to artificial light well into the evening and night. The blue light emitted by screens is particularly disruptive because it suppresses melatonin production more than other wavelengths. The result is a delayed circadian clock and reduced sleep drive at bedtime.

Modern technology has made this problem worse. We use our phones, tablets, and computers until the moment we turn off the lights. We watch television in bed. We check email during nighttime awakenings. Our light exposure patterns are completely disconnected from the natural light-dark cycle that our circadian clocks evolved to track.

The solution is not to abandon technology. It is to use technology more wisely. Blue-light blocking glasses, screen dimming software, and the simple habit of putting devices away an hour before bed can all help.

### Treating Circadian Rhythm Disorders in the Real World

The principles of treating circadian rhythm disorders are well established — timed light exposure, melatonin, and consistent scheduling. But implementing these principles in real life can be challenging.

For people with DSPD who need to wake up early for work, morning light therapy is the most effective intervention. The challenge is consistency — using the light box every morning, even on weekends, is essential for maintaining the circadian advance.

For shift workers, the challenge is even greater. Their schedules are in direct conflict with their biology. Strategic napping, caffeine timing, and light management can help, but they are partial solutions. Some shift workers find that rotating shifts are harder to manage than fixed night shifts, because the circadian clock is constantly trying to adjust.

### The Future of Circadian Treatment

New treatments for circadian rhythm disorders are being developed. Chronotherapy, which involves gradually shifting the sleep schedule in small increments over days or weeks, is an established approach. Newer approaches include the use of melanopsin agonists, which target the specific photoreceptor in the eye that mediates the light response, and wearable devices that deliver timed light pulses to shift the clock.

Digital therapeutics for circadian disorders are also emerging. Smartphone apps and wearable devices that track sleep, light exposure, and activity can provide personalized recommendations for circadian alignment. These tools make treatment more accessible and allow for more precise timing of interventions.
