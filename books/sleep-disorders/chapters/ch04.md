## Chapter 4: Sleep Apnea and Breathing Disorders

Sleep apnea is one of the most common and most dangerous sleep disorders. It is also one of the most treatable. Yet the vast majority of people who have it do not know it. Understanding sleep apnea could save your life — and it might improve the quality of your sleep more than anything else you do.

### What Is Sleep Apnea?

Sleep apnea is a condition in which breathing repeatedly stops and starts during sleep. These pauses, called apneas, can last from ten seconds to over a minute. They can occur hundreds of times per night, each one waking you briefly — often without your awareness — as your brain fights to restart breathing.

The most common form is obstructive sleep apnea, or OSA, which occurs when the muscles in the back of the throat relax excessively during sleep. This relaxation causes the soft tissue at the back of the throat to collapse and block the airway. When your brain senses that you are not getting enough oxygen, it sends a signal to wake you just enough to reopen the airway. You might gasp, snort, or make a choking sound as you resume breathing.

Central sleep apnea, or CSA, is less common. It occurs when the brain fails to send the proper signals to the muscles that control breathing. Unlike OSA, where there is a physical blockage, CSA is a failure of the respiratory control center in the brain. Complex sleep apnea, also called treatment-emergent central sleep apnea, is a combination of both types.

### The Symptoms You Might Miss

The classic symptom of sleep apnea is loud, disruptive snoring followed by episodes of silence, then a gasp or snort. But not everyone who snores has sleep apnea, and not everyone with sleep apnea snores. Other symptoms include excessive daytime sleepiness, waking up with a dry mouth or sore throat, morning headaches, difficulty concentrating, irritability, and waking up frequently during the night to urinate.

Many people with sleep apnea do not realize they have it because they do not remember waking up. Each apnea event causes a micro-arousal — a brief awakening that might last only a few seconds. These micro-arousals do not register in conscious memory, but they prevent the brain from entering or maintaining deep sleep.

### The Health Consequences

Untreated sleep apnea is linked to a long list of serious health conditions. The repeated drops in blood oxygen levels put stress on the cardiovascular system. Research has found that untreated sleep apnea increases the risk of high blood pressure, heart attack, stroke, atrial fibrillation, and heart failure. The risk is significant — people with severe untreated sleep apnea have a two to three times higher risk of stroke.

Sleep apnea is also strongly linked to type 2 diabetes. The intermittent hypoxia and sleep fragmentation caused by apnea disrupt glucose metabolism and increase insulin resistance. Treating sleep apnea has been shown to improve blood sugar control in people with diabetes.

Beyond the physical effects, sleep apnea takes a heavy toll on cognitive function and mental health. The chronic sleep deprivation caused by fragmented sleep impairs memory, attention, and decision-making. It contributes to depression and anxiety. It increases the risk of motor vehicle accidents by two to three times.

### Who Is at Risk

Certain factors increase the risk of sleep apnea. Excess weight is the strongest risk factor, particularly fat deposits around the upper airway. However, sleep apnea also affects people who are not overweight. Men are two to three times more likely to have sleep apnea than women, though the risk for women increases after menopause. Age is a factor — the risk increases with age. Family history, smoking, nasal congestion, and the use of alcohol or sedatives before bed also increase the risk.

### Diagnosis

The gold standard for diagnosing sleep apnea is a polysomnogram, or sleep study. This test records brain activity, eye movements, heart rate, breathing patterns, blood oxygen levels, and body movements during sleep. Sleep studies can be done in a sleep laboratory or at home using a portable monitoring device.

The severity of sleep apnea is measured by the apnea-hypopnea index, or AHI, which counts the number of apnea and hypopnea events per hour of sleep. An AHI of five to fifteen is considered mild, fifteen to thirty is moderate, and over thirty is severe.

### Treatment Options

Continuous positive airway pressure, or CPAP, is the most common and effective treatment for moderate to severe OSA. A CPAP machine delivers a steady stream of pressurized air through a mask that you wear over your nose, or both nose and mouth, while you sleep. The air pressure keeps your airway open, preventing collapses and allowing you to breathe normally throughout the night.

CPAP is highly effective when used consistently. It eliminates apnea events, restores normal oxygen levels, and allows you to enter deep sleep. Most people report feeling significantly better within the first few nights of treatment. However, CPAP can take some getting used to. The mask can feel uncomfortable, and the noise of the machine can be intrusive. Working with a sleep specialist to find the right mask and pressure settings makes a significant difference.

Other treatment options include oral appliances that reposition the jaw to keep the airway open, positional therapy that encourages sleeping on your side rather than your back, and in some cases, surgery to remove excess tissue from the throat. For people who cannot tolerate CPAP, these alternatives can be effective.

### Lifestyle Changes That Help

Certain lifestyle changes can reduce the severity of sleep apnea. Weight loss is the most effective — even a 10 percent reduction in body weight can significantly improve the AHI. Avoiding alcohol before bed is important because alcohol relaxes the throat muscles and worsens apnea. Sleeping on your side rather than your back can help. Quitting smoking reduces inflammation in the airway.

### When to See a Doctor

If you have been told that you snore loudly, if you wake up gasping or choking, if you are excessively sleepy during the day despite what seems like adequate sleep, or if you have risk factors for sleep apnea, it is worth discussing with a healthcare provider. Sleep apnea is a medical condition that requires professional diagnosis and treatment. Ignoring it does not make it go away — it allows the damage to accumulate.

### Reflection Questions

- Do you snore? Has anyone ever told you that you stop breathing during sleep?
- How sleepy are you during the day? Do you fall asleep easily in situations where you should be alert — reading, watching TV, driving?
- Have you ever woken up with a headache, dry mouth, or a feeling of choking?
- If you have been diagnosed with sleep apnea, what has been your experience with treatment?

### The Mask of Sleep Apnea

Sleep apnea is sometimes called the silent thief of sleep because it often goes undetected for years. Many people with sleep apnea dismiss their symptoms as normal aging, poor fitness, or just being a naturally tired person. They do not realize that their exhaustion has a specific, treatable cause.

One reason sleep apnea is so often missed is that the person experiencing it does not know it is happening. The micro-arousals that occur after each apnea event are brief enough that they do not register in conscious awareness. The person may genuinely believe they slept through the night, even though their brain was fighting to breathe every few minutes.

Bed partners are often the first to notice the signs. If your partner has told you that you snore loudly, stop breathing during sleep, or make gasping sounds, take it seriously. This is not just about noise — it is a medical red flag.

### Children and Sleep Apnea

Sleep apnea also affects children, and it often presents differently than in adults. Instead of excessive daytime sleepiness, children with sleep apnea may show hyperactivity, inattention, irritability, and behavioral problems. These symptoms can easily be mistaken for ADHD.

The most common cause of sleep apnea in children is enlarged tonsils and adenoids. Surgical removal of the tonsils and adenoids often resolves the condition. If your child snores loudly, breathes through their mouth during sleep, has restless sleep, or shows behavioral or attention problems that do not respond to other interventions, sleep apnea should be considered.

### The Emotional Impact of CPAP

Starting CPAP therapy can be an emotional experience. Some people feel a sense of relief and hope — finally, there is a solution. Others feel anxious about sleeping with a mask on their face. Some feel self-conscious about using a machine in front of a partner. All of these reactions are normal.

The adjustment period varies. Some people adapt to CPAP within a few nights. Others take weeks or months to get comfortable. The key is persistence and support. Work with your sleep specialist to find the right mask type and size. Modern CPAP masks come in many designs — nasal pillows, nasal masks, full-face masks — and finding the right one makes a significant difference.

Humidification can help with the dryness that some people experience. Pressure adjustments can make the therapy more comfortable. Gradual desensitization, where you wear the mask while awake for short periods before trying to sleep with it, can ease the transition.

### Positional Therapy for Mild Apnea

For people with mild to moderate sleep apnea that occurs primarily when sleeping on their back, positional therapy can be effective. The goal is to prevent back sleeping, which allows the tongue and soft palate to collapse backward more easily.

Positional therapy devices range from simple tennis balls sewn into the back of a pajama shirt to specialized belts and pillows that encourage side sleeping. Some newer devices use vibration sensors that alert you when you roll onto your back.

Positional therapy is not appropriate for everyone with sleep apnea. People with moderate to severe apnea or apnea that occurs in all sleep positions usually need CPAP or other interventions. But for people with milder, position-dependent apnea, it can be a viable alternative.

### The Link Between Sleep Apnea and Other Conditions

Sleep apnea does not exist in isolation. It is associated with a range of other health conditions that often co-occur. Obesity is the strongest risk factor, but the relationship is bidirectional — sleep apnea can also contribute to weight gain by disrupting metabolism and reducing energy for physical activity.

Sleep apnea is also strongly linked to atrial fibrillation, the most common type of irregular heartbeat. Treating sleep apnea in people with atrial fibrillation can improve heart rhythm outcomes. The relationship is so strong that sleep apnea screening is recommended for people with atrial fibrillation that does not respond well to treatment.

Gastroesophageal reflux disease, or GERD, is another condition that frequently co-occurs with sleep apnea. The pressure changes in the chest during apnea events can pull stomach acid into the esophagus, causing reflux. Treating sleep apnea often improves GERD symptoms.
